How to Use Moldflow Communicator: Getting the Most Out of Simulation Results Without a Moldflow License

Simulation results are only useful when the people who need to act on them can actually see and understand them. A detailed Moldflow study locked inside an analyst's workstation does not prevent a bad tooling decision — it just means the right information was not in the right hands at the right time.

Autodesk Moldflow Communicator solves this problem. It is a free standalone viewer that allows engineers, toolmakers, program managers, and customers to open, navigate, and interrogate Moldflow simulation results without owning or installing Moldflow Insight or Adviser. If your analyst is using Moldflow and you are receiving their results as PDFs or static screenshots, you are working with a fraction of the information that is available to you.

This guide explains what Moldflow Communicator is, how to install it, how to open and navigate a result file, and how to use it productively in design reviews, tooling meetings, and program decisions.

Moldflow Communicator is free to download and use. Your analyst publishes a .sdy file from their Moldflow project and sends it to you. You open it in Communicator. That is the entire distribution chain.

What Moldflow Communicator Is — and What It Is Not

Communicator is a viewer. It does not run simulations, change analysis inputs, or modify mesh geometry. Think of it the way you think of Acrobat Reader relative to Acrobat Pro — the full tool creates and edits, the viewer opens and explores.

What you can do in Communicator:

  • Open and rotate the 3D model in any orientation.
  • Switch between all result plots your analyst included in the published file.
  • Animate fill, pack, and cooling sequences.
  • Query specific nodes or regions to read exact result values.
  • Toggle layers, gates, runners, and cooling circuits on and off.
  • Cut cross-sections through the part to inspect internal results.
  • Compare results side by side if multiple scenarios were published.
  • Add markup notes and annotations directly on the model.

These capabilities are significant. A design engineer who can animate a fill sequence and query the knit line location on a specific surface can ask substantively better questions and make better decisions than one who is looking at a color-scaled screenshot with no ability to explore.

How to Get Moldflow Communicator

Communicator is available as a free download from Autodesk's website. Search for “Autodesk Moldflow Communicator” on the Autodesk website or the Autodesk App Store. There is no license fee and no subscription required. You will need to create or use a free Autodesk account to complete the download.

System requirements are modest. Communicator runs on Windows and requires a standard graphics card with OpenGL support. Most engineering workstations and many business laptops will run it without issue. Installation is straightforward and takes a few minutes.

Once installed, confirm that your Moldflow analyst knows you are using Communicator. They will need to publish their results in the .sdy format (Moldflow Study file) rather than sending screenshots or a PDF summary. The .sdy file contains the full 3D result data that Communicator needs.

Opening a Result File

When your analyst sends you a .sdy file, save it to a local folder and open Communicator. Use File → Open and navigate to the file location. Communicator will load the model geometry, mesh, and all result sets that were included in the publish.

The interface has three main regions. On the left is the Study Tasks panel, which lists the analyses that were run (Fill, Pack, Cool, Warp) and the result plots available for each. The main window in the center displays the 3D model. On the right, or in a toolbar across the top, are display controls — legend scaling, clipping planes, animation controls, and visibility toggles.

Start by rotating the model to orient yourself. Use the left mouse button to rotate, the right button or scroll wheel to zoom, and the middle button or Shift + left button to pan. These are the same navigation conventions used in most 3D CAD viewers.

Understanding the Result Plots Panel

The Study Tasks panel on the left shows the result categories your analyst published. Typical categories include:

  • Fill Time — how the melt front progresses through the cavity.
  • Pressure at End of Fill — pressure distribution at transfer point.
  • Weld Lines — locations where flow fronts converge.
  • Air Traps — regions where gas may be trapped.
  • Clamp Force — clamping force evolution through the fill and pack cycle.
  • Sink Marks — predicted surface depression from local shrinkage.
  • Warpage — total displacement and individual x, y, z component deflections.
  • Volumetric Shrinkage — local shrinkage variation that drives differential warpage.
  • Temperature at Flow Front — melt temperature as the front advances.
  • Cooling Time — time to reach ejection temperature across the part.

Click on any result name to display it on the model. The color scale legend will appear showing the range from minimum to maximum. Results that fall below or above the displayed range can be rescaled by right-clicking the legend and entering new limit values.

Animating Fill and Pack Sequences

Fill time results are most informative when viewed as an animation rather than as a static final-state plot. In the animation toolbar, click the Play button to watch the fill sequence progress from gate to last-to-fill region. You will see:

  • How the flow front advances from the gate and whether it stays balanced.
  • Where hesitation occurs — regions where the front slows as it encounters thin sections.
  • Where race tracking occurs — where flow accelerates through thick sections and arrives early at the perimeter.
  • Exactly where and when weld lines form, and whether they are consolidating at high or low temperature.

Use the playback speed controls to slow the animation at critical moments. If the fill sequence shows a balanced front reaching the perimeter evenly, that is a positive sign. If one region fills significantly earlier than another while sharing a cavity boundary, that asymmetry will drive differential packing and is a warpage risk worth discussing with your analyst.

Querying Specific Locations

To read the exact result value at a specific surface location, use the Query tool in the toolbar — it may appear as a crosshair or probe icon depending on the Communicator version. Click any point on the model surface and a tooltip will display the result value at that node, along with the node ID and coordinates.

This is useful when you need to report a specific number — for example, confirming that warpage at a critical datum surface is below a tolerance threshold, or verifying that volumetric shrinkage at a thick boss is within acceptable range. Screenshots with the queried value visible can be included directly in design review documentation.

Using the Clipping Plane

Thin-wall parts are easy to interpret on the surface, but thicker structural components, ribbed parts, and multi-cavity tools often require a cross-section view to understand what is happening through the thickness. The clipping plane tool lets you cut through the model at any angle and position.

To activate the clipping plane, look for the section or clip icon in the display toolbar. You can drag the plane interactively, set it to a specific coordinate, or align it to a flat surface. Common uses include:

  • Inspecting volumetric shrinkage through a rib cross-section to predict sink severity.
  • Viewing cooling temperature gradients through part thickness.
  • Checking whether fiber orientation shifts from skin to core in the section direction.
  • Verifying that runner and gate geometry is represented correctly in the model.

Comparing Scenarios

If your analyst published multiple design scenarios — for example, two gate location options, or the original cooling layout versus a proposed improvement — Communicator can display them side by side. Use the Window → Tile option to arrange multiple open study windows in a split view.

When comparing scenarios, synchronize the model orientation across windows so that you are looking at the same region from the same angle. Most versions of Communicator have a sync view option that links the camera position across tiled windows. This makes direct visual comparison much faster than toggling between separate windows.

Design reviews become much more productive when all stakeholders can rotate the same model themselves, query a value they are unsure about, and animate the fill to understand why a gate location was chosen. Communicator turns a presentation into a conversation.

Adding Markup and Annotations

Communicator includes basic annotation tools that let you add notes directly to the 3D view. You can place text labels on the model, draw leader lines pointing to specific features, and capture annotated views as images for meeting notes or design review records.

A practical workflow for design reviews: before the meeting, the responsible engineer opens the .sdy file and places annotations on any region they want to discuss — flagging a weld line location, marking the highest warpage zone, or noting a pressure concern near a thin feature. Annotated views are saved and used as the starting point for the meeting agenda. This preparation dramatically shortens the explanation time and keeps discussions focused on decisions rather than orientation.

Warpage Results: Reading Them Correctly

Warpage results are among the most misread outputs in Moldflow. The default “total displacement” plot shows the magnitude of deflection at each point, which is useful for identifying where the part moves the most. But total displacement does not tell you the direction of the movement.

To understand warp direction, switch from the total displacement plot to the component plots: X displacement, Y displacement, and Z displacement. Each shows how much the part deflects in that coordinate direction. A part that warps symmetrically but in opposite directions across its length will show high total displacement but balanced X or Y components — and may be far less problematic than a part with asymmetric unidirectional deflection.

When reviewing warpage results in a design meeting, always ask your analyst to walk through the component plots, not just the total displacement plot. The question is not only “how much does it move?” but “which way does it move, and does that direction affect the assembly interface?”

Cooling Results: What to Look For

Cooling analysis results in Communicator include mold temperature distribution, part temperature at ejection, and cycle time contributions. The most useful result for identifying quality risk is the part temperature at ejection, viewed at the end of the cooling phase.

Look for regions where part temperature at ejection is significantly higher than the surrounding areas. These hot spots are where the part is not adequately cooled before it leaves the mold and where post-ejection creep or warpage is likely. Localized hot spots over ribs, gates, and thick section transitions are common targets for cooling circuit improvements.

If the analysis includes a conformal cooling alternative, compare the temperature distribution uniformity between the conventional and conformal scenarios side by side. Improved temperature uniformity at ejection typically predicts reduced warpage — and the comparison in Communicator makes that relationship visual and immediate.

Sharing Results With Stakeholders Who Do Not Have Communicator

Not every stakeholder in a program review will have Communicator installed. For those audiences, use the File → Export Image function to capture high-resolution screenshots of specific result plots and annotated views. Export the fill animation as a video file and include it in the meeting slide deck.

When preparing images for non-technical audiences — program managers, customers, finance reviewers — prioritize clarity over completeness. A single annotated warpage plot showing measured deflection at the critical interface, with an arrow pointing to the affected datum, communicates the risk better than six technical plots that require CAE training to interpret.

Getting the Most From Your Analyst

Communicator is most powerful when the analyst and the review team work together to define what will be published in the .sdy file before the analysis is run. A file that contains only the plots the analyst thought were relevant may not include the result a toolmaker needs to evaluate a cooling channel change. Define the result set in advance: agree on which analyses will be run, which plots will be published, and what the acceptance criteria are for each critical output.

When you receive the .sdy file, open it before the formal review meeting and make a short list of questions. Note any regions where results are worse than expected, any plots where the color scale seems compressed in a way that may be hiding variation, and any scenarios you want to compare. Bring those notes into the review. An analyst who receives specific, informed questions produces much better follow-up work than one who is asked generic questions after a passive slide presentation.

Final Takeaway

Moldflow Communicator removes the barrier between simulation results and the people who need to act on them. It costs nothing, requires no engineering software background to install, and transforms a static report into an interactive engineering tool that any stakeholder can use.

If you are on a program that involves Moldflow analysis, request the .sdy file from your analyst, install Communicator, and spend thirty minutes exploring the results yourself before the next design review. The questions you will be able to ask — and the decisions you will be able to make — will be qualitatively different from what is possible with a PDF summary. That is the practical value of getting simulation results into the right hands.
