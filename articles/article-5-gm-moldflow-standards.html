What General Motors Requires in Moldflow and Why Moldflow Needs to Be Done by Approved Analysts

General Motors does not accept Moldflow results the way most of the industry has treated them for decades — as a checkbox, a colorful fill animation to satisfy a customer request, or a late-stage gate-location exercise. GM requires a structured, documented, phased simulation program governed by published standards, executed by analysts who have been reviewed and formally approved through GM's own internal process.

If you supply plastic components to GM, or if you are a molder or tool shop serving Tier 1 suppliers on a GM platform, this framework is not optional. Understanding what the standards actually demand — and why analyst qualification is central to the whole system — is essential for anyone serious about participating in GM's supply chain without costly surprises.

GM's standards exist because the industry, left to its own devices, was producing wildly inconsistent Moldflow results — some rigorous, some dangerously misleading. The standards impose a floor of quality that protects programs, budgets, and launch timing.

Why GM Created Its Own Moldflow Standards

GM conducted a broad survey of Moldflow practice across its supply chain and found a fragmented landscape. Some suppliers had expert-certified analysts running thorough, physics-driven analyses. Others assigned Moldflow duties to whoever had a seat license, regardless of training or experience. The outputs of these two groups were being treated as equivalent, even when one was rigorous and one was not.

The consequences showed up at launch. Warpage that simulation had predicted — and flagged — was ignored because engineers and program managers lacked the context to interpret the results. Other programs had weld line and air trap defects that a proper analysis would have caught months earlier, at a fraction of the tooling change cost. In still other cases, material data used in the simulation was outdated or incomplete, making the predictions unreliable regardless of the analyst's skill.

GM responded by building its own standards, its own approved analyst program, and its own curated material database. The two governing documents are GMW 16355, which defines the injection molding simulation requirements, and GMW 16365, which governs the T1 tryout protocol. Both are available through the IHS / ats.com standards portal. Together they define what an acceptable Moldflow program looks like at every stage of a vehicle development program.

The Three Moldflow Milestone Stages

GM's Global Vehicle Development Process has defined milestones that map directly to Moldflow deliverables. The three stages are MF0, MF1, and MF2, and each has a different scope, a different level of model maturity, and a different set of expected outputs.

MF0 happens at the SIVER stage — early concept, well before mold construction. The goal is a 30,000-foot view: is this part manufacturable as currently designed? Analysis at this stage covers filling, packing, and warpage at a conceptual level. There is no cooling design to evaluate yet, but the fill pattern, gate zone viability, and potential warpage risk are assessed to influence part design while changes are still inexpensive.

MF1 arrives at the ESPER stage. The part design is more developed, and the analysis now adheres fully to GMW 16355. Gate architecture, runner concepts, and filling patterns are established here. This is the stage where design changes informed by simulation are still feasible without major schedule or cost impact.

MF2 occurs at the IVER stage, when mold construction is underway. Cooling designs are available, manifold and hot runner layouts are finalized, and full-blown simulation of the complete mold system is performed. At this stage, the analysis is a verification and optimization exercise — the window for fundamental design changes has largely closed.

This phased structure is not bureaucratic overhead. It reflects a hard-learned truth: the earlier simulation influences design decisions, the cheaper and more effective those decisions are. By the time a mold is being cut, fixing a warpage driver costs an order of magnitude more than it would have at MF0.

Material Data: The Foundation Everything Else Depends On

The most overlooked requirement in GM's standards — and arguably the most important — is material characterization. Moldflow is built on physics models that require eleven distinct data sets to run with full accuracy. These include viscosity data, pvT data (pressure, volume, temperature relationships), thermal conductivity, heat capacity, shrinkage and warpage coefficients, and fiber orientation data for filled materials, among others.

Moldflow's commercial database contains over 11,000 material entries, but not all of them are fully characterized for all eleven data sets. Some entries are years or decades old and may not reflect the current grade of resin from the material supplier. GM's standard explicitly prohibits the use of material database files older than three years unless they have been independently verified against current material properties.

To address this at scale, GM built its own proprietary material database, funded the characterization of approved materials, and made that database available exclusively to GM-approved analysts. When you look at material quality indicators in Moldflow's database and see gold-rated entries across filling, packing, and warpage categories, those are the materials meeting the standard.

Using an incomplete material file does not just reduce accuracy — it can make predictions dangerously wrong. A case with missing juncture loss data projected 10,000 PSI injection pressure. The correct material data showed 23,000 PSI. The molder would have arrived at the press unable to fill the part.

In another documented case, a supplier ran Moldflow analysis with inadequate shrinkage data, predicted 3mm of warpage, and proceeded to cut the mold. Actual warpage was nearly 6mm. By the time GM's own material database became available and the analysis was rerun, the cavity side of the mold had already been scrapped. A new manifold was built, gate count was increased to three, and the program paid for an entirely avoidable correction. The material file was the failure point — not the mold design, not the processing.

Mesh Quality and Simulation Setup Standards

Beyond material data, GMW 16355 defines expectations for model setup and mesh quality. Analysts must make informed choices between midplane, dual-domain, and 3D mesh types depending on part geometry and analysis objectives. Runner and gate geometry must be modeled accurately, not approximated with simplified inputs. Cooling circuits must be represented with sufficient fidelity to yield meaningful thermal predictions.

Mesh density is not a detail to minimize for solve speed. A documented example from the webinar series supporting GM's standards showed a chrome appearance part where a weld line did not appear in simulation until the mesh was refined to 3.3 million elements. At coarser resolution, the convergent flow-front behavior that produces the knit was not resolved. The analyst would have signed off on a geometry that fails visually in production.

Getting mesh quality right is part of the analyst approval criteria. Applicants must demonstrate that they understand what constitutes a good mesh, where refinement is required, and how to balance element count against solve time without sacrificing predictive accuracy.

What a GM-Approved Analyst Actually Is

The approved analyst designation is not a title you can self-certify or purchase. It is earned through a defined process managed by GM's Manufacturing Polymer Engineering Group and it has explicit prerequisites.

The first requirement is CAE background. GM does not approve analysts whose only qualification is holding a Moldflow seat license. Simulation work without engineering judgment — without the ability to interpret what results mean in a physical manufacturing context — is the root cause of the low-quality Moldflow outputs the standards were created to eliminate.

The second requirement is Autodesk Moldflow Expert Certification. There are three certification levels — Associate, Professional, and Expert. The approved analyst program requires progression through all three. Expert certification is not a rubber stamp. It requires demonstrated proficiency in simulation setup, result interpretation, and troubleshooting across a range of real-world injection molding scenarios.

After certification, an analyst submits an application to GM and must demonstrate three specific capabilities:

  • The ability to identify appearance and dimensional defects in simulation results — not just produce outputs, but read them correctly.
  • The ability to determine root causes of warpage and defect behavior, distinguishing between filling, packing, cooling, and material-driven contributors.
  • The ability to propose and evaluate solutions across all corrective levers — tooling design, processing, material selection, and part geometry.

An analyst who can only run a fill analysis and produce a fill-time animation fails this criteria. The standard requires a full-systems view of the injection molding process and the simulation tools that represent it.

Why Analyst Quality Changes Program Outcomes

The approved analyst requirement exists because simulation is only as valuable as the judgment that interprets it. Two analysts can run the same geometry with the same material in the same software version and reach completely different conclusions — about gate viability, about warpage risk, about what process window exists and how robust it is.

An inexperienced analyst may miss a problematic knit line because the mesh was too coarse to resolve it. They may not recognize that a pvT data gap is making their shrinkage predictions unreliable. They may not think to vary processing conditions systematically to understand sensitivity. They may interpret a Moldflow fill animation as validation of the design when it is actually a warning.

GM's requirement for stakeholder engagement makes this point concrete. The standard requires that the Moldflow analyst engage with the GM product design engineer, the manufacturing engineer, the part supplier, the tooling source, the material supplier, and the hot runner supplier before the analysis scope is set. Why? Because without those conversations, the analyst does not know where gates are permitted, where knit lines are cosmetically or structurally unacceptable, what the critical tolerances are, or what the toolmaker can and cannot accommodate.

A gate location that looks excellent from a filling and warpage perspective may land on a Class A surface, require a slide that creates a tooling conflict, or place a knit line through a load-bearing boss. The analyst who runs simulation in isolation and presents results without that context is producing engineering theater, not engineering insight.

Approved analysts do not just run simulations — they run them in context, with the right inputs, and with the ability to close the loop between predicted results and what actually happens when the mold runs.

The Cost of Ignoring Standards

The financial argument for GM's standards is not theoretical. The webinar series documenting GM's requirements includes specific cost examples from real programs. A liftgate trim component with Moldflow-predicted warpage that was dismissed by the program team accumulated tens of thousands of dollars and dozens of days in gap and flush correction after launch. Another part required two windage iterations at over $64,000 and 40-plus days of trial-and-error tooling modification because the simulation-predicted warpage was not addressed proactively.

These costs are not unusual. They are representative of what happens across the industry when simulation is treated as a formality rather than a decision-support tool. The standards exist to interrupt that pattern — to create a consistent baseline where analysis is taken seriously because it was done rigorously, by a qualified analyst, with verified inputs, and with documented outputs at each program milestone.

GM's Broader Goal: Kaizen Across the Supplier Community

The deepest objective behind GM's Moldflow standards is continuous improvement at a community scale. By establishing a documented baseline, GM can track improvement over time — not just within programs, but across the entire network of approved analysts and suppliers. Analysts who go through the approval process and work within the standard get better at their craft faster, because the structure requires them to document, verify, and correlate their predictions against actual results.

This improvement loop also feeds back to the software developer. Autodesk can see where predictions are systematically off, where characterization data gaps are causing consistent errors, and where user expectations are revealing holes in capability. Standards, at their best, are not bureaucracy — they are the mechanism by which an entire industry raises its floor.

What This Means If You Are a Molder or Tool Shop on a GM Platform

If you are producing plastic components for GM programs, the practical implications are straightforward. Your Moldflow analysis must be performed by a GM-approved analyst. If you do not have one in-house, you must engage one. The analysis must follow the phased structure defined in GMW 16355. Material data must meet the verification requirements. Mesh quality and model setup must meet the standards' criteria. And the outputs at each milestone must be documented in the format the standard requires.

This is also a business differentiation opportunity. Suppliers who have invested in GM-approved analyst capability are not just checking a compliance box — they are demonstrating a level of engineering rigor that reduces program risk, builds trust with the OEM, and wins repeat business because launches go better. The cost of doing Moldflow correctly is almost always lower than the cost of the problems that result from doing it poorly.

Final Thought

General Motors' Moldflow standards are one of the most thorough OEM simulation frameworks in the automotive industry. They exist because the consequences of poor simulation practice are measurable, expensive, and repeatable across programs. The approved analyst requirement is not a gatekeeping exercise — it is the most direct way to ensure that the results delivered at each program milestone are credible enough to drive real decisions.

If you are evaluating Moldflow support for a GM program, the single most important qualification to verify is analyst approval status. Everything else — software version, hardware capacity, turnaround speed — is secondary to the question of whether the person running the simulation has the judgment, the training, and the GM endorsement to deliver results you can actually rely on.
